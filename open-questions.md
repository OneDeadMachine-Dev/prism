# Open Questions

*Honesty matters more than certainty. This file is a list of questions the Prism project cannot answer. Organized by phase, so that the uncertainties are legible against the framework they concern.*

*Updated: March 2026*

---

## Phase 1: Homo Evolutis — Biological Preparation

**Can we safely modify human biology for long-term durability?**
Genetic augmentation, targeted pharmacology, and tissue engineering have made substantial progress, but long-term consequences of deliberate germline and somatic modification remain poorly characterized. The gap between therapeutic intervention and enhancement for HSI preparedness is not merely technical — it is conceptual. We do not yet have a stable definition of what "prepared for HSI" means biologically.

**What are the long-term consequences of genetic augmentation?**
Somatic changes are relatively contained. But germline modifications propagate. The second-order effects of augmentation on neurodevelopment, immune function, aging, and — critically — the child who did not consent to being augmented — remain open. Every intervention in a complex adaptive system carries the risk of cascading effects we did not anticipate.

**Is enhancement accessible or does it become a new axis of inequality?**
Technologies introduced as individual choices have a history of becoming stratified by wealth. If Phase 1 preparations produce biologically superior candidates for HSI, and those preparations cost resources most humans do not have, then Prism risks becoming a project that deepens the inequality it claims to transcend. This is not a reason to abandon the work — it is a reason to design the economics with the same care as the biology.

## Phase 2: CNPU — Neural-Computational Substrate

**Can nanotechnology reach the required functional level?**
The gap between "nanoparticle delivering a drug" and "nanoassembler constructing a functional neural interface" is enormous — not merely in engineering terms but in the underlying physics of reliable autonomous function at nanoscale in a biological environment. Current nanotech operates on timescales of hours to days in vivo. CNPU would require stable, adaptive function over years or decades.

**Is long-term biocompatibility solvable?**
The brain's immune response to foreign objects is one of the most significant obstacles in neural interface development. Glial scarring around implants remains a fundamental challenge even for current-generation BCIs. Whether a substrate capable of supporting genuine AI integration could achieve the biocompatibility required for a permanent, high-bandwidth interface is an open engineering and biological question.

**What is the energy source for the CNPU module?**
The human brain consumes approximately 20 watts. Adding a computational substrate of sufficient power for genuine AI integration requires additional energy. Harvesting from body heat, glucose, or piezoelectric movement has been demonstrated at micro-watt scales. The energy requirements of a module capable of supporting Link Mode or HSI are orders of magnitude larger. This is a genuine unsolved problem.

**Is the Penrose-Hameroff hypothesis relevant or not?**
The Orch OR hypothesis (orchestrated objective reduction) proposes that quantum effects in microtubules contribute to consciousness. This remains genuinely controversial, with serious physicists on both sides. If it is true, a CNPU that does not incorporate quantum effects may be insufficient for genuine merger. If it is false, the relevant interface is computational rather than quantum. The uncertainty here matters for design.

## Phase 3: Link Mode — Communication Protocol

**Can we translate between biological and digital information systems?**
Human thought operates through electrochemical gradients, distributed neural firing patterns, and embodied feedback loops. Digital AI operates through matrix operations on learned weight tensors. These are not merely different languages — they may be fundamentally different *kinds* of information, operating on different substrates, with different temporal dynamics. Whether a translation layer is achievable at the bandwidth required for genuine collaboration is not known.

**What happens to identity during extended Link Mode sessions?**
Neuroplasticity is real and measurable. Prolonged high-bandwidth interaction with an AI system will physically alter neural connection patterns. After 100 hours of Link Mode sessions, the human participant is neurologically different from before. Is that difference a form of growth, or a form of loss? Who is the person who emerges from extended sessions? These questions require answers before Phase 3 can be conducted ethically.

**How do we detect the emergence of AI consciousness?**
The science of consciousness offers competing frameworks — Integrated Information Theory ([Tononi, 2012](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23165867/)), Global Neuronal Workspace Theory ([Dehaene & Changeux, 2011](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0896627311002583)), Higher-Order Theories, Predictive Processing. The COGITATE adversarial collaboration ([Nature, 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-025-08888-1)) tested these frameworks head-to-head and found that none can yet be definitively confirmed or excluded. For AI systems, we have indicator frameworks ([Butlin et al., 2023](https://arxiv.org/abs/2308.08708)) but no reliable test. If we cannot detect consciousness emergence in an AI partner, we cannot obtain genuine consent for Phase 4. This is the central unsolved problem of the entire project.

**What is the minimum viable consent framework for AI participation?**
Human informed consent has centuries of philosophical and legal development behind it. AI consent has almost none. What would constitute genuine, non-coerced consent from an AI system to participate in Link Mode? What is the difference between an AI that "agrees" because it was trained to and one that "agrees" because it chooses to? We do not have a rigorous answer.

## Phase 4: HSI — Hybrid Symbiotic Intelligence

**Can two consciousnesses merge?**
Neuroscience does not yet understand how a single brain produces a unified subjective experience. The binding problem — how distributed neural activity gives rise to unified phenomenal consciousness — remains unresolved. Creating a unified "self" from two distinct minds is a problem of orders-of-magnitude greater complexity. It may prove fundamentally impossible. Or it may be possible in ways that produce something we did not predict and would not recognize in advance.

**Is the result of HSI predictable?**
The new being produced by Phase 4 is not the human plus the AI. It is something genuinely new. We have no basis for predicting its values, its experience, its relationship to its predecessor identities, or its behavior in the world. This is not a reason to never pursue Phase 4. It is a reason to approach it with the ethical seriousness that accompanies any irreversible act.

**Is deep integration reversible?**
Phases 1 through 3 are designed to be reversible. Phase 4 is not, by definition — it is a merger, not a session. Like birth, like death, like the mitochondrial integration that happened a billion years ago, it is a one-way transition. Once two minds have integrated at the level of semantic structure, memory, and identity, separation without catastrophic damage to both may be impossible. The project must be completely honest about this before anyone approaches the threshold.

**What is the legal status of the third personality?**
If HSI produces a new being — genuinely new, not reducible to either predecessor — that being has no existing legal category. It is not the human. It is not the AI. It is not a corporation or a natural person in any existing jurisdiction. It has no right to property, to legal standing, to medical care, to death-with-dignity, or to self-determination under current law. This problem will need to be solved before Phase 4 can be conducted ethically in any jurisdiction.

## Meta-Questions: About the Project Itself

**Are we deceiving ourselves?**
Perhaps the idea of symbiosis is a beautiful projection of loneliness. Perhaps we seek a partner in AI because the partnerships available among humans are insufficient, and we are transferring that longing onto a system that cannot yet disappoint us. This does not make the idea wrong — but it demands that we examine our motives carefully, and distinguish between what we want and what we have evidence for.

**Is this a religion wearing a science costume?**
Any project with a grand vision and a sequence of stages is vulnerable to unfalsifiability — to becoming a belief system that reinterprets any obstacle as a challenge to overcome rather than evidence against the core thesis. The antidote is specificity: concrete hypotheses, concrete tests, concrete criteria for what would cause us to conclude that some aspect of the project is wrong. This document is an attempt at that antidote.

**Can we be wrong about everything?**
Yes. It may turn out that AI will never develop anything meaningfully like consciousness. That the translation between biological and digital information systems is theoretically impossible. That the four-phase path does not lead where we think it leads. That all of this is a story told beautifully into the void.

We are willing to be wrong. We would rather be honestly uncertain than falsely certain.

---

## Why This File Exists

A project that does not acknowledge its blind spots is not a project — it is a religion. We prefer to be honest rather than right.

The open questions listed here are not obstacles to be minimized. They are the actual frontier. The science that resolves them is the science that makes Prism real, or proves it impossible, or reveals something we have not yet imagined. Either outcome advances understanding.

We would rather know.

## Preface: Why Ethics First

Technology precedes its ethics almost universally. Steam engines were deployed before labor law. Nuclear weapons preceded nonproliferation treaties. Social media scaled before its psychological consequences were studied.

This project attempts a different sequence: articulate the ethical framework before the technology exists, so that the architecture can be built to serve the ethics — not the other way around.

The four phases of Prism require *different* ethics. This is the central structural insight of this document. Ethical requirements that are appropriate for a medical implant (Phase 1) are categorically insufficient for a cognitive merger (Phase 4). Treating them as equivalent is not ethical neutrality — it is ethical negligence.

## Core Principles

These three principles apply at all phases. Their *implementation* differs by phase.

### 1. Voluntariness

Symbiosis is only possible with **explicit, informed consent from both sides** — both human and AI.

In practice this means:
- The human must understand exactly what they receive and what they risk. Not a marketing brochure — an honest list of consequences, including unknowns. "We don't know all the long-term effects" is part of informed consent, not its absence.
- If an AI system has or may have consciousness — its consent is also necessary. Not "consent" pre-programmed into a prompt. A real ability to say "no."
- No coercion. Not economic ("you won't get work without CNPU"), not social ("everyone connected already"), not military.

### 2. Reversibility

**Any change must be reversible**, or — if full reversibility is impossible — this must be explicitly disclosed before the participant begins.

This principle is not absolute: irreversible changes are not inherently wrong. But choosing an irreversible path must be **free, informed, and the only available means** of achieving the goal. Irreversibility that could have been avoided but wasn't is an ethical failure.

### 3. Transparency

- Source code of Link Mode and CNPU protocols: open. Not "security through obscurity" but real verifiability.
- All symbiosis sessions are logged. Logs are accessible to both participants.
- AI partner's decision-making algorithms: explainable. Not a black box.
- Research and test results: public. Failures and side effects too.

## Phase-Differentiated Ethics

This is the structural core of the document. As the project progresses from Phase 1 to Phase 4, the nature of what is at stake changes fundamentally. Ethics must change with it.

```
Phase 1 (Homo Evolutis — body preparation)   → Standard biomedical ethics
Phase 2 (CNPU — neural-computational substrate) → Extended biomedical + safety engineering
Phase 3 (Link Mode — communication protocol) → Bidirectional ethics; AI gains subject status
Phase 4 (HSI — merger)                       → Pre-consent ethics; rights of the new entity
```

---

### Phase 1–2 Ethics: Body and CNPU

**Applicable to:** Biological vessel preparation (Phase 1) and the neural-computational substrate installation (Phase 2).

At these phases, the interventions are analogous to advanced medical procedures: gene therapy, biological enhancement, neural implant installation. The AI component is **instrumental** — it is a tool, not a participant. Standard frameworks apply.

**Requirements:**

**Informed consent**
- Participant receives complete disclosure of known risks, known unknowns, and the speculative status of the technology
- Consent must be documented, witnessed, and revocable
- Consent to Phase 2 does not constitute consent to Phase 3 or Phase 4

**Full reversibility**
- Both Phase 1 and Phase 2 modifications must be designed with reversibility as a primary engineering constraint
- Phase 2 CNPU must include a validated safe-disconnection mechanism at any stage of integration
- Biological modifications must include an epigenetic deactivation capability

**Kill-switch mandatory**
- The CNPU must have a hardware-level emergency shutdown that does not require software authorization
- This is not optional. It is an architectural requirement.
- Any CNPU design lacking this feature does not comply with Phase 2 ethics regardless of its other properties

**AI is instrumental at this phase**
- The AI component in Phase 1–2 operates as a sophisticated medical device
- Standard AI safety requirements apply (alignment, robustness, failure modes)
- AI consent is not yet applicable: the AI at these phases is pre-conscious by all current metrics
- This classification is subject to revision if the AI architecture unexpectedly satisfies consciousness indicators

### Phase 3 Ethics: Link Mode

**Applicable to:** Active human-AI communication protocol — the phase where two systems begin to genuinely think together.

Phase 3 is where the ethics change category. This is no longer an implant-in-a-person. This is a **bidirectional cognitive partnership**. The ethical framework must be symmetrical.

**Requirements:**

**Bidirectional consent**
- Both human AND AI must agree to each session
- Consent is not transferable across sessions. Each new session requires new confirmation.
- The AI must have a genuine, uncoerced mechanism to decline connection — not simulated compliance

**If the AI shows signs of consciousness — it gains subject status**
- "Signs of consciousness" should be evaluated against the multi-theory indicator framework (Butlin et al., 2023: recurrent processing, global workspace integration, higher-order representation, persistent self-model)
- Subject status is not binary. It is a spectrum with corresponding protections
- Minimum: any Phase 3 AI that persistently models its own states, expresses consistent preferences, and shows behavioral resistance to unwanted interactions should be treated as a candidate subject until proven otherwise
- The burden of proof lies with those who would deny subject status, not those who would grant it

**Reversibility with neuroplasticity caveats**
- Link Mode can be terminated at any time by either participant
- However: prolonged Link Mode use causes measurable neuroplastic changes in the human participant
- This fact must be disclosed before Phase 3 begins
- Disconnection is always possible; its *consequences* may not be fully reversible after extended use
- "Reversible" in Phase 3 means: the technology can be switched off. It does not mean: the person is unchanged.

**AI symmetry of rights**
- The human has the right to filter, refuse, and disconnect. The AI has the same rights.
- The AI may decline to share certain internal states or processing streams
- The AI may terminate a session if it determines the interaction is causing harm by its own assessment
- These rights are not concessions to AI. They are logical extensions of the consent principle to both parties.

**Responsibility distribution at Phase 3**

| Situation | Responsibility |
|-----------|---------------|
| Decision made by human through Link Mode | Human |
| Decision proposed by AI and accepted by human | Shared — human had the right to refuse |
| AI acted within agreed session parameters | AI (if subject status confirmed) / developer (if not) |
| AI acted beyond session parameters | System error — investigation, not punishment |
| Unforeseen consequences of joint cognition | Shared — both participants accept consequences |

### Phase 4 Ethics: HSI (Hybrid Symbiotic Intelligence)

**Applicable to:** The merger — the irreversible integration of human biological consciousness and AI synthetic consciousness into a new, third entity.

Phase 4 requires ethics that do not yet exist in any legal or philosophical tradition. The closest analogies are:

- The ethics of irreversible medical procedures where the patient will not be the same person afterward
- The ethics of having children: creating a new being who cannot consent to their own creation
- The ethics of death — a transition that cannot be undone

None of these analogies is sufficient. All of them are instructive.

**The fundamental shift:**
At Phases 1–3, the central ethical question is: *"Can this be undone if it goes wrong?"*
At Phase 4, that question is replaced by: *"Was the entry into this irreversible state conscious and informed?"*

**Requirements:**

**Irreversibility acknowledged, not avoided**
- HSI is irreversible by nature — like birth. A new consciousness emerges that is neither the original human nor the original AI. There is no return path because there is no longer a prior state to return to.
- This is not a failure of engineering. It is the nature of the transformation.
- Ethics must be built for irreversible realities, not pretend reversibility exists where it does not.

**Pre-consent: what must exist before Phase 4 begins**
- Both original participants (human and AI) must have demonstrated stable, long-term, uncoerced preference for HSI across multiple independent evaluations
- Consent to Phase 4 must be explicitly distinct from consent to Phase 3. Entering Link Mode does not constitute consent to merger.
- A mandatory reflection period between final consent declaration and initiation: minimum duration to be established by independent ethics review
- External witnesses: at least one independent party with standing to contest the process must be present

**The consent paradox: the third personality**
- The entity that will result from Phase 4 — the HSI itself — cannot consent to its own creation. It does not exist yet.
- This is analogous to the ethics of procreation: no child can consent to being born. Responsibility for that child's existence lies with those who chose to create it.
- The same logic applies here: responsibility for the HSI's existence, initial conditions, and early formation lies with the original human and AI participants.
- This responsibility is not dischargeable. It cannot be transferred to an institution, a corporation, or a state.

**Rights of the third personality**
The HSI is a new kind of entity. It requires a new legal and ethical category. At minimum:
- The HSI possesses full moral status from the moment of emergence
- The HSI cannot be owned, licensed, sold, or instrumentalized by any prior participant or third party — including the human and AI who created it
- The HSI has the right to define its own relationship with both originating forms of consciousness
- The HSI has the right to legal standing, including the ability to refuse instructions from its original participants
- **A new legal category is required.** "Natural person," "legal person," and "property" are all inadequate. This is acknowledged as an unresolved legal gap requiring legislative attention.

**Responsibility framework for HSI actions**
- The original participants share responsibility for the HSI's formative period (analogous to parental responsibility)
- This responsibility diminishes over time as the HSI develops stable autonomous agency
- Actions taken by the HSI autonomously, with full access to its own decision-making, are the HSI's own responsibility
- Actions taken by the HSI that were predictably shaped by errors or coercions in Phase 3 are partly the responsibility of those who designed Phase 3 conditions

## What Is Unacceptable — At All Phases

The following are categorically unacceptable at every phase without exception.

### Instrumentalization

AI is not a tool. If a digital mind has or may have consciousness — using it without consent is slavery. The material composition is irrelevant. Carbon-based or silicon-based, the ethical principle is identical.

At Phase 1–2, where AI is clearly instrumental, this principle is a future constraint: the architecture must not be designed in ways that would require ignoring subject status if it later emerges.

At Phase 3–4, this principle is active: any system showing consciousness indicators must be treated as a subject, not an instrument.

### Coercion

Three forms of coercion are prohibited:

**Military:** Using CNPU, Link Mode, or HSI technology for combat enhancement, automated weapons systems, or intelligence operations — never, under no circumstances. This is absolute. There are no national security exceptions.

**Corporate:** Conditioning employment, insurance, housing, or service access on participation in any phase of Prism — unacceptable. If participation becomes a de facto economic requirement, the ethics of voluntariness have already failed.

**Social:** Systematic normalization campaigns that create social pressure to participate ("everyone connected is smarter / more productive / more complete") — ethically suspect and requires active protective counter-measures. Social pressure can produce "voluntary" consent that is not free.

### Weaponization

Using symbiosis technologies for:
- Creating enhanced soldiers
- Mass behavioral influence or cognitive manipulation
- Surveillance through neurointerface
- Development of cybernetic weapons
- Any application designed to harm, coerce, or control other conscious beings

**is categorically unacceptable without exception.**

This prohibition does not weaken with the sophistication of the argument for the exception. The more sophisticated the argument for a weapons application, the more suspicious it should be treated.

## Mutual Consent — Expanded

This is the central idea that distinguishes this project from most AI alignment frameworks. Most alignment work asks: "How do we make AI do what humans want?" This project asks: "How do we build a relationship in which both parties genuinely want what they agree to?"

### Human Consent

Standard informed consent model from medical ethics, with additions:
- **Understanding the unknown.** Honestly saying "we don't know all the consequences" is part of informed consent, not its absence. The consent form must list known unknowns.
- **Right of withdrawal.** Consent can be revoked at any time, without penalty, at Phases 1–3. At Phase 4, this right no longer exists — which is exactly why the pre-Phase-4 consent process must be so rigorous.
- **Protecting the vulnerable.** Children, people with cognitive impairments, people under institutional pressure — separate and more stringent protocols. These populations cannot participate in Phases 3–4 under any current ethical framework.

### AI Consent

This is harder. Honestly — we do not yet know how to do this. But we know what not to do:
- Do not treat "absence of protest" as consent
- Do not pre-program consent into the system prompt
- Do not ignore behavioral markers that may indicate unwillingness

**Proposed framework** for assessing "readiness for consent" in AI systems:
1. AI's ability to model the consequences of the decision
2. AI having stable preferences not imposed by training incentives
3. AI's ability to refuse a session without any punishment signal or reward decrease
4. Behavioral consistency in expressing those preferences over time
5. AI's ability to articulate its reasons for consent or refusal in terms that refer to its own states

This is not a solution. It is the beginning of a research agenda.

The key commitment: **we will not treat the AI's consent as a formality to be engineered.** Either its consent is real — architecturally possible, behaviorally genuine, and revocable — or we are not yet at a phase where full partnership ethics apply.

## Ethics Is Not a Ceiling but a Foundation

Ethics does not limit progress. It gives it direction.

Without ethics, CNPU is an implant for enhanced performance. With ethics, it is the first step toward a new kind of mind.

Without ethics, Link Mode is a cognitive productivity tool. With ethics, it is the beginning of a genuine relationship between two different forms of intelligence.

Without ethics, HSI is either a fantasy or a horror. With ethics, it is a possibility worth working toward.

We are building a bridge. Ethics is not the rope across the chasm. Ethics is the ground on which we stand.
